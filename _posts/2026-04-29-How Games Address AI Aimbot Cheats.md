With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence technology, AI applications have permeated every aspect of social life, and the gaming industry is no exception; various AI models and tools have significantly boosted efficiency. However, advanced technology can also be misused, and some members of the Game Black/Gray Industry have exploited AI technology to develop various AI cheats, with AI aimbots being a prime example.<!-- more --> 

![315_21](/assets/res/2026/4291.png) 

AI aimbot cheats are a new type of cheating method that has emerged in the shooting game genre in recent years. Unlike traditional cheats that directly modify game memory data, AI aimbot cheats use computer vision technology to identify enemy positions solely based on the game’s visuals. They then utilize AI models to control mouse movement and firing, thereby enabling “auto-aim” and “auto-fire” functions.

Observation shows that the core execution process is as follows: the game screen is captured in real time using software screenshots or video capture cards, and the captured images are preprocessed and converted into usable data structures and formats; It then leverages object detection models such as YOLO and HRNet to accurately identify the outlines, joint points, and head coordinates of in-game characters, outputting the pixel positions of enemies within milliseconds; finally, using methods such as keyboard and mouse emulation, it controls the crosshair to automatically move toward the target and performs recoil control and shooting operations, while also simulating the subtle tremors and delays inherent in human operation to evade the feature detection of anti-cheat systems.

![315_21](/assets/res/2026/4292.png) 

This technical feature gives AI aimbots a level of stealth and versatility far surpassing that of traditional cheats, which not only significantly increases the difficulty of anti-cheat measures but also poses a major threat to the competitive integrity of first-person shooter games.

The issue of cheats in first-person shooter games is no longer merely a technical battle; it has evolved into an industry-wide war involving massive illicit profits. Reports indicate that if the number of cheat reports for a first-person shooter game increases by more than 30% month-over-month, player retention rates could plummet by 15% to 25%.

Studies show that the churn rate for players who encounter Cheats is 3 to 5 times higher than that of regular users, and the cost of re-engaging a churned user can be more than five times higher than the cost of acquiring a new one. This vicious cycle—where Cheats thrive while regular players leave—is severely eroding the very foundation of first-person shooter games.
